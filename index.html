Component 18 — SECAMB Associate Ambulance Practitioner

Complete revision: bullet summaries + expanded answers (AAP perspective)

  • Indications: When basic measures fail to maintain airway/ventilation or when rapid vascular access is required in life-threatening situations.
  • Contraindications: Local factors (infection, fracture), relative patient factors and where less invasive methods suffice.
  • Advanced airway: Secures airway, supports ventilation and reduces aspiration risk but needs high skill and monitoring.
  • IV cannulation: First-line vascular access for medication, fluids and sampling; check for local contraindications.
  • IO access: Time-critical alternative with high success when IV access is unobtainable.
  • Fluid infusion: Restores circulating volume but risks overload and electrolyte disturbance if misapplied.
  • Monitoring: Continuous observation with numerical recordings drives decisions and escalation.
  • Senior clinician: Responsible for advanced interventions; AAPs work under delegation and local SOPs.
  • Infection control & documentation: Aseptic technique, correct labelling and accurate records protect patients and staff.
  • Teamwork & escalation: Clear communication, checklists and predefined plan B/C are essential to safety.
  • Scope: Deliver care within AAP competencies and local SECAMB protocols under delegation.
  • Preparedness: Maintain equipment readiness (airway, IV/IO, fluids, monitoring).
  • Airway support: Use basic adjuncts, assist advanced procedures and confirm placement where delegated.
  • Vascular access: Prepare, assist, and insert peripheral lines when authorised; support IO insertion.
  • Infusions: Set up and monitor fluid therapy per senior clinician instructions.
  • Monitoring: Continuous observations, early detection of deterioration and clear escalation.
  • Documentation: Accurate records of time, device, size, volumes and patient response.
  • Infection prevention: Aseptic technique, hand hygiene and safe disposal of sharps/waste.
  • Communication: Handover, concise updates and assertive concerns for patient safety.
  • Professional development: Maintain CPD, scenario practice and competency sign-off.
Quick points (indications, contraindications, pros & cons)
  • Indications: Inability to protect airway (e.g., GCS ≤8), respiratory arrest, refractory hypoxia, severe facial/neck trauma, airway obstruction.
  • Contraindications: Technique-specific — e.g., some supraglottic devices where aspiration risk is very high; consider alternatives.
  • Advantages: Secure airway, better oxygenation/ventilation, reduced aspiration risk, facilitates controlled ventilation and drug delivery.
  • Disadvantages: Requires high skill, potential for hypoxia during attempts, airway trauma, unrecognised oesophageal intubation.
  • Note: Always prepare plan B/C, capnography confirmation and chest rise/auscultation are essential.
Advanced airway management is used when simple measures (head-tilt, jaw-thrust, oropharyngeal/nasopharyngeal airways, bag-valve-mask ventilation) are insufficient to maintain oxygenation or protect the airway. Common methods include supraglottic airway devices and endotracheal intubation. For the AAP, the role is supportive: prepare equipment, pre-oxygenate, apply monitoring (pulse oximetry, capnography, ECG, NIBP), and assist the senior clinician. The main benefits are a reliably secured airway and improved ability to ventilate and protect against aspiration. Risks include procedural complications, delays while attempting definitive airway, and physiological responses (tachycardia, hypertension). Good preparation, team coordination, and immediate readiness to use alternative devices (e.g., supraglottic device if intubation fails) reduce harm and improve success.
Quick points (indications, contraindications, pros & cons)
  • Indications: Immediate drug administration, fluid resuscitation, blood sampling, and as a route for meds en route to hospital.
  • Contraindications: Local infection, burns, lymphoedema, thrombosed veins, fistula site; avoid affected limb post-mastectomy if instructed.
  • Advantages: Titrated drug delivery, quick onset, easy sampling and infusion control.
  • Disadvantages: Difficult in collapsed veins, risk of extravasation, phlebitis, infection and accidental arterial puncture.
  • Note: Document size, site, time and secure with appropriate dressing/label.
IV cannulation provides direct access to the circulation for medications, crystalloid fluids and blood sampling — a core prehospital intervention. For an AAP, duties range from equipment preparation to insertion if competence and delegation allow, or assisting a senior clinician. Key practical steps include vein selection, aseptic skin prep, correct cannula selection and secure fixation. Regular patency checks and observation for swelling, redness or loss of flow are essential. In hypovolaemic or shocked patients veins may be collapsed, increasing difficulty; in such cases escalate to a senior clinician early and consider intraosseous access if indicated. Accurate documentation (gauge, site, time, number of attempts) supports later care continuity and governance.
Quick points (indications, contraindications, pros & cons)
  • Indications: Cardiac arrest, profound shock, severe dehydration, or when IV access is unobtainable and immediate access is needed.
  • Contraindications: Fracture of target bone, previous IO in same bone, local infection/osteomyelitis, some paediatric growth plate concerns.
  • Advantages: Rapid, reliable access with high success rates even in collapsed veins.
  • Disadvantages: Pain on infusion (especially without analgesia), limited dwell time, risk of extravasation and rare compartment syndrome.
  • Note: Secure, label and document insertion site, needle size and time of insertion.
IO access places a specialised needle into the medullary cavity (commonly proximal tibia, humeral head, or distal femur in children) to deliver drugs and fluids into the central circulation. It is indicated when IV attempts have failed or would unduly delay resuscitation. IO provides very rapid access and allows most resuscitation drugs and fluid boluses to be given effectively. As an AAP you will usually prepare the IO kit, help position and stabilise the limb, provide analgesia where permitted, and assist the senior clinician with insertion. After insertion you must flush to confirm patency, secure the device, label it clearly and monitor the limb. Stop infusion and escalate if swelling, increasing pain or compromised distal perfusion suggest extravasation or compartment syndrome.
Quick points (indications, contraindications, pros & cons)
  • Indications: Hypovolaemia, sepsis (initial resuscitation), dehydration, permissive fluid therapy per local protocol.
  • Contraindications/Cautions: Heart failure, renal failure, pulmonary oedema risk — balance benefit vs risk.
  • Advantages: Restores preload, supports blood pressure and tissue perfusion; carries meds when used as carrier.
  • Disadvantages: Risk of fluid overload, dilutional coagulopathy with large volumes, electrolyte disturbances.
  • Note: Monitor vitals and respiratory signs; document volumes and rate.
Fluid infusion is used to restore or maintain intravascular volume and support organ perfusion. In prehospital care, isotonic crystalloids (e.g., 0.9% saline or balanced solutions) are commonly used for initial resuscitation of hypovolaemia or sepsis as per clinical guidance. As an AAP you prepare fluids and giving sets, prime lines and connect them aseptically, set or calculate infusion rates as directed by the senior clinician, and closely monitor the patient for response and adverse effects. Key monitoring parameters include blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory rate and oxygenation, urine output when available, and peripheral perfusion. Escalate immediately if signs of fluid overload (increasing work of breathing, crepitations, falling oxygen saturations) or infusion site problems are observed.
Quick points
  • Definition: Registered clinician with extended scope and decision-making authority (paramedic, advanced paramedic, specialist paramedic, doctor).
  • Responsibility: Final clinical decisions, authorisation of advanced interventions and clinical governance.
  • Relationship to AAP: Delegates tasks within competency; provides supervision and escalation guidance.
  • Key: Ensure you know who the senior clinician is on each job and clarify delegation.
  • Note: Senior clinicians provide training, support and clinical oversight on complex cases.
A senior clinician is the individual with overall clinical responsibility and an extended scope of practice for an incident — typically a paramedic, advanced paramedic, specialist paramedic or other registered practitioner with authorisation for advanced skills. They assess the patient’s clinical needs, make decisions about advanced interventions (airway, IO, medication choices), and provide clinical leadership for the crew. For the AAP, the senior clinician is the person to whom you report assessment findings, from whom you take delegated tasks, and to whom you escalate problems or deterioration. Knowing chain of command, local standing orders and when to call for senior input are essential components of safe, effective prehospital care.
Quick points (duties)
  • Prepare equipment: ET tubes, supraglottic devices, laryngoscope, suction, capnography and BVM.
  • Monitoring: Apply and watch pulse oximetry, capnography, ECG and NIBP.
  • Assist: Hand instruments, provide BVM ventilation and apply cricoid pressure when directed.
  • Confirm: Help confirm tube position using capnography, bilateral chest rise and auscultation.
  • Document: Time, device, size, confirmation method and physiological responses.
In practice an AAP supports advanced airway management by making the procedure efficient and safe. Responsibilities include preparing and checking all airway devices, positioning the patient for optimal view, pre-oxygenation, and setting up monitoring equipment. During an attempt you will assist the senior clinician by providing bag-valve-mask ventilation, handing tools, suctioning secretions and applying adjuncts as requested. Once the airway is secured you help confirm placement using capnography, chest rise and auscultation, secure the device and ensure continuous monitoring of oxygenation and ventilation. You must document the procedure in detail and be ready to implement contingency plans (e.g., supraglottic device) if intubation is unsuccessful. Clear communication, assertive escalation if problems arise, and adherence to local protocols protect patient safety.
Quick points (duties)
  • Prepare: Gather cannulae, dressings, flush syringes, tourniquet and sharps container.
  • Aseptic technique: Hand hygiene, skin prep and sterile handling.
  • Insertion or assistance: Insert within scope or assist senior clinician; label site with time and size.
  • Monitor: Check for patency, infiltration, bleeding or infection.
  • Escalate: Stop attempts after agreed number and seek senior clinician for alternatives (IO).
An AAP’s role in IV cannulation includes preparing the sterile tray, assessing veins and selecting an appropriate site, performing or assisting the insertion if within local competency, and securing and labelling the cannula. Aseptic technique and good ergonomics improve success and reduce complications. After securing the cannula, flush to confirm patency, record gauge, site and time, and monitor for swelling, pain, redness or loss of flow. If access proves difficult after the agreed number of attempts, you must escalate promptly and help prepare for alternative access such as intraosseous. Clear documentation and handover ensures continuity of care and supports clinical governance.
Quick points (duties)
  • Preparation: Assemble IO device, flush syringes, adhesive dressings and labels.
  • Support insertion: Position and stabilise limb, provide analgesia per local policy.
  • Confirm & secure: Flush, secure needle hub and label with time/site/needle size.
  • Monitor limb: Watch for swelling, pain escalation, decreased distal perfusion.
  • Escalate: Stop infusion and inform senior clinician immediately if complications suspected.
IO insertion is commonly performed by a senior clinician but the AAP fulfils vital supporting duties. These include preparing a sterile field and the correct needle size, positioning the patient and limb for optimal access, and providing analgesia if within scope and indicated. After the senior clinician inserts the IO needle, flush to confirm patency and secure the device to prevent dislodgement. Label with insertion time and site, and set up infusions or medications as directed. Regular inspection of the limb is necessary to detect extravasation, swelling or compromised perfusion; any of these findings require immediate cessation of infusion and escalation to the senior clinician for assessment and removal.
Quick points (duties)
  • Prepare fluids: Check type, expiry, integrity and correct prescription or instruction.
  • Set up: Prime giving set, remove air and secure connections.
  • Administer: Set rate (calculated or pump), document volumes and start/stop times.
  • Monitor: Observe vitals, respiratory signs and infusion site for complications.
  • Escalate: Stop infusion and notify senior clinician if overload or adverse reaction.
When delivering fluid therapy the AAP must ensure the correct fluid is selected and bag integrity confirmed. Prepare the giving set using aseptic technique, prime to remove air, and connect while securing the cannula or IO access. Set or calculate the infusion rate as directed by the senior clinician and document the volume given and time of initiation. Monitor the patient for therapeutic response (improved perfusion, rise in blood pressure) and for complications such as increasing work of breathing, pulmonary oedema, or local extravasation. Report and escalate any adverse signs immediately. Accurate record keeping and clear communication at handover are critical so receiving teams know exactly what fluids were given and why.
Quick points
  • Early detection: Continuous monitoring identifies deterioration early, enabling timely interventions.
  • Assess treatment response: Numerical changes show whether interventions (fluids, airway, meds) are effective.
  • Documentation: Accurate, time-stamped observations support clinical decisions and handover.
  • Communication: Clear, concise escalation to senior clinicians improves patient safety.
  • Legal & governance: Monitoring records provide an audit trail for quality and safety reviews.
Continuous monitoring — observing airway, breathing, circulation, level of consciousness and oxygenation — is fundamental in prehospital care because patient conditions can change abruptly. Regular, numerical recording (e.g., SpO₂, respiratory rate, blood pressure, heart rate) lets clinicians assess trends and the immediate effect of treatments. For an AAP, being the constant presence at the bedside means you are often first to notice deterioration. Prompt, structured reporting (what changed, when, and the measured values) to the senior clinician enables faster escalation and can be life-saving. Good monitoring practice also ensures accurate handover to receiving teams and supports incident documentation for clinical governance and learning.
Quick points
  • Safeguarding: Always consider vulnerability, consent, capacity and adult/child protection issues.
  • Infection control: Hand hygiene, aseptic technique for IV/IO/airway procedures and correct PPE.
  • Sharps & waste: Immediate disposal in compliant containers and correct clinical waste segregation.
  • Documentation: Record indications, times, device sizes, confirmation method, volumes and patient response.
  • Handover: Use SBAR or structured reports and ensure transfer of responsibility is clear and documented.
Safeguarding and infection prevention are integral to prehospital practice. Always assess for immediate safeguarding concerns (risk to children, vulnerable adults) and follow local reporting procedures. Infection control measures — including hand hygiene, aseptic technique during cannulation/IO insertion, PPE use and decontamination of equipment — reduce harm. Correct disposal of sharps and contaminated waste protects staff and patients. Thorough documentation is not optional: note the indication, site, device size, insertion time, confirmation methods (e.g., capnography), volumes given and the patient’s physiological response; include verbal handover content and receiving clinician name. Structured handover (SBAR) and clear documentation support continuity, clinical governance and legal accountability.
